# Research Digest — 2026-05-25

*Weekly digest for Marcus Frid, Osteoarchaeological Research Laboratory, Stockholm University. Focus: Late Iron Age Scandinavia, zooarchaeology, mortuary practice, human-animal relations.*

## Burial Archaeology and Mortuary Practice

**The Late Iron Age Mound Raknehaugen in Norway: A Ritual Response to the Sixth-Century Crisis** / *Lars Gustavsen (2026) — European Journal of Archaeology* / Reinterprets Scandinavia's largest prehistoric mound not as a royal burial but as a communal ritual monument erected in response to the AD 536 dust-veil climatic catastrophe, directly engaging questions of collective mortuary practice and landscape in Vendel-period Norway.
Link: https://doi.org/10.1017/eaa.2025.10026

**Monuments as Active Cooperation: Crisis, Mound Construction, and Society in Scandinavia in the 6th Century AD** / *Andreas Ropeid Sæbø (2025) — Journal of Social Archaeology* / Argues that large-scale mound construction during the 6th-century crisis reflects joint ritual action, communal solidarity, and group identity rather than elite power, offering a bottom-up reading of Iron Age monumental burial.
Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/14696053251366275

**Ritual Practice at Late Iron-Age and Viking-Age Cemeteries in Norway: The Mortuary Houses From Skeiet, Vinjeøra** / *Raymond Sauvage & Richard Macphail (2024) — Medieval Archaeology 68(1)* / Reports three Iron Age and Viking Age mortuary structures (AD 500–950) that functioned as interfaces between the living and the dead, with micromorphological evidence linking the earliest phase to the transition from cremation to inhumation.
Link: https://doi.org/10.1080/00766097.2024.2347753

**Narratives of Inequality: Towards an Archaeology of Structural Violence in Late Iron Age Scandinavia** / *Ben Raffield, Christina Fredengren & Anna Kjellström (2024) — Archaeological Dialogues 30(2): 137–162* / Applies Johan Galtung's concept of structural violence to bioarchaeological and textual data, foregrounding enslaved and low-status individuals at the margins of elite Iron Age burial discourse.
Link: https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/archaeological-dialogues/article/narratives-of-inequality-towards-an-archaeology-of-structural-violence-in-late-iron-age-scandinavia/CCBE7389CA33F41ADEA074DE81BD6170

## Norse Religion and Ritual

**The Demise of Norse Religion: Dismantling and Defending the Old Order in Viking Age Scandinavia** / *Olof Sundqvist (2024) — Walter de Gruyter [Open Access monograph]* / Comprehensive actor-centred study of the Christianisation process (c. 950–1100), examining how Viking Age kings dismantled indigenous religious structures while pagan chieftains resisted; authored at Stockholm University's Department of Ethnology, History of Religions and Gender Studies.
Link: https://library.oapen.org/handle/20.500.12657/87862

**Merovingian or Vendel Period? The Norwegian-Swedish Divide** / *Mats Skare (2024) — Frühmittelalterliche Studien 58(1): 77–94* / Critically revisits the historiographical and political contexts behind the divergent Norwegian ('Merovingian') and Swedish ('Vendel') terminology for AD 550–750, with an updated synthesis of diagnostic material including animal style, brooches, and weapons.
Link: https://doi.org/10.1515/fmst-2024-0002

## Cremation Archaeology

**In Sheep's Clothing? Humans and Other-Than-Humans in Iron Age Cremation Assemblages in North Spånga, Sweden** / *Fredrik Fahlander (2024) — Current Swedish Archaeology 32: 99–128* / Examines animal remains in a Late Iron Age grave-field in Stockholm and argues that skinned ovicaprids — represented by skulls and distal limb elements — were deployed during cremation as apotropaic agents rather than simple food offerings, blurring the companion/commodity divide.
Link: https://doi.org/10.37718/CSA.2024.5

## Multispecies Archaeology and Birds

**Zooarchaeology of Managed, Captive, Tame, and Domestic Birds: Shifts in Human–Avian Relationships** / *L. Yeomans (2025) — Journal of Archaeological Research* / Comprehensive review of archaeological evidence for intensifying human control over birds across time, mapping the spectrum from wild exploitation to full domestication and providing a methodological framework directly applicable to avian assemblages in Iron Age and Viking Age burial contexts.
Link: https://doi.org/10.1007/s10814-024-09206-5

**Feral Ecologies of the Human Deep Past: Multispecies Archaeology and Palaeo-Synanthropy** / *Shumon T. Hussain (2024) — Journal of the Royal Anthropological Institute 30: 1062–1084* / Articulates a multispecies archaeological framework centred on the synanthropic niche, challenging wild/domestic binaries and arguing that past animal agency co-shaped the archaeological record from an early date — a theoretical grounding relevant to interpreting ambiguous faunal assemblages in Iron Age Scandinavia.
Link: https://doi.org/10.1111/1467-9655.14152

## Zooarchaeology

**Animals in Iron Age Mortuary Rites: Traditions, Transitions, and Transformations in Rogaland, SW Norway** / *Arild Klokkervoll (2025) — PhD thesis, University of Stavanger (UiS no. 877)* / Synthesises all documented faunal mortuary depositions from 128 grave assemblages in Rogaland (500 BC – AD 1050), identifying distinct regional traditions and tracing the religious and relational dimensions of human-animal mortuary assemblages across the Iron Age.
Link: https://uis.brage.unit.no/uis-xmlui/handle/11250/3209504

**Animal Bones From Iron Age Settlements in Scania, Southern Sweden: A Database From the Sheep Ahoy Project** / *Stella Macheridis (2024) — Journal of Open Archaeology Data 12(11): 1–7* / Open dataset of osteological data from 130 assemblages across 101 settlement sites in Scania, generated by the Sheep Ahoy project's investigation of ovine husbandry and wool production in the Late Iron Age.
Link: https://doi.org/10.5334/joad.126
