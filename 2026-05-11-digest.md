## Burial Archaeology and Mortuary Practice

**Ritual Practice at Late Iron-Age and Viking-Age Cemeteries in Norway: The Mortuary Houses From Skeiet, Vinjeøra** / *Sauvage R & Macphail R (2024) — Medieval Archaeology* / Three successive underground mortuary houses (AD 575–940s) in central Norway reveal sustained ritual engagement between the living and their dead, with animal and food sacrifices, across the transition from cremation to inhumation. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1080/00766097.2024.2347753

**The Impact of the 6th Century Crisis — Exploring Burials as a Proxy for Population Dynamics in Iron Age Scandinavia** / *Loftsgarden K & Iversen F (2024) — Norwegian Archaeological Review 57(2):105–125* / Quantitative analysis of ~7,000 dated burials documents a >75% post-6th-century decline in South Norway, linking mortuary data to climate-driven population collapse triggered by the AD 536/540 volcanic eruptions. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1080/00293652.2024.2412018

**Narratives of Inequality: Towards an Archaeology of Structural Violence in Late Iron Age Scandinavia** / *Raffield B, Fredengren C & Kjellström A (2024) — Archaeological Dialogues 30(2)* / Integrates bioarchaeological and mortuary evidence to identify enslaved and marginalised individuals in Late Iron Age burial contexts, foregrounding the osteological record as a witness to structural violence. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1017/S1380203823000107

**Merovingian or Vendel Period? The Norwegian-Swedish Divide** / *Anonymous (2024) — Frühmittelalterliche Studien (De Gruyter)* / Analyses the historiographical and cultural reasons why Norway and Sweden use different terminology for the AD 550–800 period, with implications for cross-national comparative burial studies. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1515/fmst-2024-0002

## Norse Religion and Ritual

**The Late Iron Age Mound Raknehaugen in Norway: A Ritual Response to the Sixth-Century Crisis** / *Gustavsen L (2026) — European Journal of Archaeology* / Reinterprets Scandinavia's largest mound as a communal ritual monument constructed in the wake of the AD 536 dust-veil catastrophe rather than a royal tomb, connecting monumental architecture to crisis cosmology. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1017/eaa.2025.10026

**Monuments as Active Cooperation: Crisis, Mound Construction, and Society in Scandinavia in the 6th Century AD** / *Sæbø AR (2025) — Journal of Social Archaeology* / Argues that mound-building at Hundorp and comparable sites constituted collective communal action and ritual feasting, reframing Iron Age monumentality as cooperative social practice rather than elite display. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/14696053251366275

## Cremation Archaeology

**In Sheep's Clothing? Humans and Other-Than-Humans in Iron Age Cremation Assemblages in North Spånga, Sweden** / *Fahlander F (2024) — Current Swedish Archaeology 32(1):99–128* / Close zooarchaeological analysis of a Late Iron Age graveyard in Stockholm distinguishes between whole-animal and skinned-animal patterns of deposition, arguing for active, generative roles of animals — including sheep pelts — within the cremation ritual sequence. / Link: https://doi.org/10.37718/CSA.2024.5

**Using ATR-FTIR, Analytical Colour and Mercury for Unravelling the Cremation Ritual of Tyresta Viking Age Burial Mound (South-Central Sweden)** / *[authors not retrieved] (2026) — Archaeological and Anthropological Sciences* / Geochemical analysis of 107 cremated bone samples from a Viking Age mound burial containing human, dog, horse and cat remains reveals differential burning temperatures between species and elevated ante-mortem mercury in the human individual, opening new avenues for multi-species cremation analysis. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1007/s12520-026-02411-x

## Multispecies Archaeology and Birds

**Feral Ecologies of the Human Deep Past: Multispecies Archaeology and Palaeo-Synanthropy** / *Hussain S (2024) — Journal of the Royal Anthropological Institute* / Proposes palaeo-synanthropic niches — the grey zone where wild and domestic converge — as a productive model system for multispecies archaeology, dismantling the wild/domestic binary that has constrained interpretation of commensal and liminal animals in the archaeological record. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1111/1467-9655.14152

**Zooarchaeology of Managed, Captive, Tame, and Domestic Birds: Shifts in Human–Avian Relationships** / *Yeomans L (2025) — Journal of Archaeological Research* / Comprehensive review of the spectrum of human control over birds from wild exploitation through full domestication, with a synthesis of zooarchaeological indicators useful for interpreting avian assemblages in funerary and settlement contexts. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1007/s10814-024-09206-5

**Why Has Avian Archaeology Not Yet Emerged?** / *[author not retrieved] (2025) — Biosemiotics* / Argues that avian archaeology has been theoretically foreclosed by anthropology's framing of the human-animal boundary, and proposes biosemiotics as the necessary theoretical foundation for studying bird agency and materiality in the archaeological record. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1007/s12304-025-09631-z

## Zooarchaeology

**Animals in Iron Age Mortuary Rites: Traditions, Transitions, and Transformations in Rogaland, SW Norway** / *Klokkervoll A (2025) — PhD thesis, University of Stavanger (UiS no. 877)* / Comprehensive synthesis of all documented faunal mortuary depositions from Iron Age Rogaland (500 BC–AD 1050) traces long-term shifts in which animal taxa and body parts were selected for burial, and what this reveals about changing human-animal ontologies across the Iron Age. / Link: https://uis.brage.unit.no/uis-xmlui/handle/11250/3209504

**Animal Bones From Iron Age Settlements in Scania, Southern Sweden: A Database From the Sheep Ahoy Project** / *[multiple authors] (2024) — Journal of Open Archaeology Data* / Open dataset of zooarchaeological assemblages from Iron Age settlement sites in Scania, enabling large-scale comparative analysis of animal husbandry and exploitation patterns in southern Scandinavia. / Link: https://doi.org/10.5334/joad.126
