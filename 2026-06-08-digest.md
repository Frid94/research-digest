# Research Digest — 2026-06-08

## Burial Archaeology and Mortuary Practice

**Ritual Practice at Late Iron-Age and Viking-Age Cemeteries in Norway: The Mortuary Houses From Skeiet, Vinjeøra** / *Raymond Sauvage & Richard I. Macphail (2024) — Medieval Archaeology* / Excavation of three successive AD 500–950 wooden mortuary structures at a central Norwegian cemetery, documenting a ritual building type distinct from graves and embedded in Late Iron Age and Viking Age funerary practice. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1080/00766097.2024.2347753

**Three Dogs from the Late Iron Age Boat Grave Cemetery at Gamla Uppsala Prästgården, Sweden** / *Christopher Nichols (2024) — European Journal of Archaeology* / Osteological analysis of dogs deposited in Vendel/Viking Age boat graves at Gamla Uppsala confirms a preference for sighthound breeds consistent with Vendel and Valsgärde, illuminating animal roles in high-status Late Iron Age burial rites. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1017/eaa.2023.40

**The Herlaugshaugen Ship Burial: Closing the Gap Between the East Anglian and Scandinavian Ship Burial Traditions** / *Geir Grønnesby et al. (2026) — Antiquity* / Publication of the 2023 re-excavation of Herlaugshaugen (Leka, Norway) confirming Scandinavia's oldest securely dated ship burial (c. AD 700) in a Merovingian tradition bridging East Anglian and later Scandinavian examples. / Link: https://doi.org/10.15184/aqy.2026.10330

**Viking Age Ship Graves as Part of a Multifaceted Repertoire of Performative Burial Customs** / *Julie Lund (2025) — Norwegian Archaeological Review* / Theoretical reappraisal situating ship burials within a broader spectrum of performative Viking Age mortuary acts, relevant for contextualising the full Late Iron Age burial repertoire across Scandinavia. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1080/00293652.2025.2556779

**Multi-Isotope Variation Reveals Social Complexity in Viking Age Norway** / *Lisa Mariann Strand, Sam Leggett & Birgitte Skar (2022) — iScience* / Multi-isotope (δ¹⁸O, δ¹³C, δ¹⁵N) analysis of human remains from Viking Age graves across Norway challenges assumptions about gender and mobility, contributing to osteoarchaeological interpretation of burial assemblages. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.isci.2022.105225

## Norse Religion and Ritual

**The Demise of Norse Religion: Dismantling and Defending the Old Order in Viking Age Scandinavia** / *Olof Sundqvist (2024) — De Gruyter (Open Access monograph)* / Comprehensive synthesis of pre-Christian ritual leadership, cultic practice, and the Christianisation process in Scandinavia, integrating archaeological and textual evidence. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1515/9783111198750

**Reflections on Posthuman Ethics: Grievability and the More-than-human Worlds of Iron and Viking Age Scandinavia** / *Marianne Hem Eriksen & Kevin Kay (2022) — Cambridge Archaeological Journal* / Theorises human-animal entanglement and ethical frameworks in Iron and Viking Age Scandinavia, bridging mortuary archaeology with posthumanist theory. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1017/S0959774321000561

**The Well and Rituals in Everyday Life — Three Iron Age Wells Excavated in Trøndelag, Norway** / *Magnar Mojaren Gran (2024) — Journal of Wetland Archaeology* / Animal bones and wooden objects filling three Iron Age wells in central Norway are interpreted as votive depositions at liminal wetland boundary sites. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1080/14732971.2024.2411798

## Cremation Archaeology

**In Sheep's Clothing? Humans and Other-Than-Humans in Iron Age Cremation Assemblages in North Spånga, Sweden** / *Fredrik Fahlander (2024) — Current Swedish Archaeology* / Investigates co-mingled human and faunal remains (sheep, horse, dog, cat) in Late Iron Age cremation graves (c. AD 400–1050) at a Swedish grave-field using an ontological approach to the role of animals in cremation ritual. / Link: https://doi.org/10.37718/CSA.2024.5

**Dividing Time — An Absolute Chronological Study of Material Culture from Early Iron Age Urnfields in Denmark** / *Helene Agerskov Rose & John Meadows (2024) — PLOS ONE* / First large-scale radiocarbon and Bayesian study of 111 cremation burials across three Danish urnfields, establishing a revised absolute chronology for the Pre-Roman to Early Iron Age transition. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0300649

**Sr Analyses from Only Known Scandinavian Cremation Cemetery in Britain Illuminate Early Viking Journey with Horse and Dog across the North Sea** / *Tessi Löffelmann et al. (2023) — PLOS ONE* / Strontium isotope analysis of cremated human and animal remains from Heath Wood demonstrates Baltic Shield origins for some individuals, showing Scandinavian cremation practice exported during the Viking Age. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0280589

**Cemetery at Store Frigård, Bornholm: Society, Exchange and Alliance Systems in the Baltic Area at Early Iron Age** / *Tine Trolle et al. (2025) — Antiquity* / Study of Bornholm's largest Early Iron Age cremation cemetery (1,256 cremation graves, 500 BC – AD 400) argues the site served as a supra-regional Baltic exchange node. / Link: https://doi.org/10.15184/aqy.2025.35

## Multispecies Archaeology and Birds

**Feral Ecologies of the Human Deep Past: Multispecies Archaeology and Palaeo-Synanthropy** / *Shumon T. Hussain (2024) — Journal of the Royal Anthropological Institute* / Articulates the theoretical framework of multispecies archaeology and palaeo-synanthropy, key methodological reading for human-animal entanglement studies. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1111/1467-9655.14152

**Between Life and Death: Waterfowl in Viking Age Funerary Practices** / *Klaudia Karpińska (2023) — in: Animals and Animated Objects in the Early Middle Ages, Brepols* / Analysis of waterfowl skeletal remains in Norwegian and Swedish Viking Age graves; a core reference for avian zooarchaeology in Scandinavian burial contexts. / Link: ISBN 978-2-503-60140-3 (peer-reviewed book chapter)

**Biomolecular Evidence Reveals Mares and Long-Distance Imported Horses Sacrificed by the Last Pagans in Temperate Europe** / *Katherine M. French et al. (2024) — Science Advances* / aDNA and strontium isotopes reveal the sex and geographic origin of sacrificed horses in Baltic Late Viking Age cemeteries, directly relevant to animal burial and ritual practice in Scandinavian mortuary contexts. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.ado3529

**Human-Beaver Cohabitation in the Early and Mid-Holocene of Northern Europe: Re-visiting Mesolithic Material Culture and Ecology through a Multispecies Lens** / *Shumon T. Hussain & Nathalie Ø. Brusgaard (2024) — The Holocene* / Empirical multispecies case study from Northern Europe, methodologically relevant to zooarchaeological interpretation of interspecies cohabitation and ecological entanglement. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/09596836231200444

## Zooarchaeology

**The Phenomenon of Primary and Secondary Animals within Iron Age Deposits** / *Pernille Bangsgaard & Pernille Pantmann (2022) — Danish Journal of Archaeology* / Defines and analyses structured deposition of primary and secondary animal remains at Iron Age ritual sites in Denmark; a foundational typological framework for ritual faunal analysis. / Link: https://doi.org/10.7146/dja.v10i0.125982

**Targeted Radiocarbon Dating of Animal Bones from Ritual Wetland Depositions in Early Iron Age (Denmark)** / *Jacob Kveiborg & Jesper Olsen (2023) — Journal of Archaeological Science: Reports* / 154 AMS dates from 10 Danish Iron Age wetland sites reveal complex, long-lived biographies of ritual animal bone deposition at each location. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jasrep.2022.103766

**Killing Cattle — Age Selection of Cattle at Iron Age Central Places in Third–Eleventh Century AD Sweden Based on Tooth Wear** / *Maria Vretemark & Ola Magnell (2022) — Environmental Archaeology* / Mortality profiles from Helgö, Gamla Uppsala, Uppåkra, and Stanstorp reveal regional patterns in cattle age selection at Iron Age central places, linking subsistence with possible ritual feasting. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1080/14614103.2022.2151723

**Animal Bones From Iron Age Settlements in Scania, Southern Sweden: A Database From the Sheep Ahoy Project** / *Stella Macheridis (2024) — Journal of Open Archaeology Data* / Open-access dataset of 130 faunal assemblages from 101 Scanian Iron Age settlement sites; a primary resource for subsistence zooarchaeology in southern Sweden. / Link: https://doi.org/10.5334/joad.126
