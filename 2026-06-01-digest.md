## Burial Archaeology and Mortuary Practice

**The Late Iron Age Mound Raknehaugen in Norway: A Ritual Response to the Sixth-Century Crisis** / *Lars Gustavsen (2026) – European Journal of Archaeology* / Challenges the royal-burial interpretation of Scandinavia's largest prehistoric mound, arguing via LiDAR and dendrochronology (c. AD 551) that it was a communal ritual response to a catastrophic landslide and the Dust Veil climate crisis — directly relevant to monumentality and mortuary practice in pre-Viking Scandinavia. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1017/eaa.2025.10026

**Ritual Practice at Late Iron-Age and Viking-Age Cemeteries in Norway: The Mortuary Houses From Skeiet, Vinjeøra** / *Raymond Sauvage & Richard I. Macphail (2024) – Medieval Archaeology 68(1)* / Reports underground wooden mortuary structures (c. AD 500–950) with animal sacrifices and food offerings, illuminating ritualised interaction between the living and the dead across the Iron Age–Viking Age transition in Norway. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1080/00766097.2024.2347753

**The Impact of the 6th Century Crisis – Exploring Burials as a Proxy for Population Dynamics in Iron Age Scandinavia** / *Kjetil Loftsgarden & Frode Iversen (2024) – Norwegian Archaeological Review 57(2), pp. 105–125* / Analyses nearly 7,000 dated burials to demonstrate a >75% post-crisis decline in burial frequency in South Norway, linking the 536/540 CE volcanic events to demographic collapse and the onset of the Merovingian/Vendel period. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1080/00293652.2024.2412018

**Monuments as Active Cooperation: Crisis, Mound Construction, and Society in Scandinavia in the 6th Century AD** / *Andreas Ropeid Sæbø (2025) – Journal of Social Archaeology* / Reframes 6th-century Scandinavian mound-building as collective ritual solidarity in the face of climate crisis, drawing on the archaeology of emotions and joint ritual action — a productive theoretical companion to the Raknehaugen and Vinjeøra papers above. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/14696053251366275

**Merovingian or Vendel Period? The Norwegian-Swedish Divide** / *Mats Skare (2024) – Frühmittelalterliche Studien 58(1), pp. 77–94* / Examines why Norwegian and Swedish archaeologists use different period labels for AD 550–750/800, asking whether the divide reflects genuine material-culture differences or historiographical habit — essential reading for anyone working across the Norwegian–Swedish late Iron Age. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1515/fmst-2024-0002

## Cremation Archaeology

**Dividing Time — An Absolute Chronological Study of Material Culture from Early Iron Age Urnfields in Denmark** / *(2024) – PLOS ONE* / Applies Bayesian chronological modelling and radiocarbon dating (including wood-age offset corrections) to 111 cremation burials from three Danish urnfields, producing the first large-scale absolute chronology for Early Iron Age cremation assemblages in the region. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0300649

**Animals in Iron Age Mortuary Rites: Traditions, Transitions, and Transformations in Rogaland, SW Norway** / *Arild Klokkervoll (2025) – PhD thesis, University of Stavanger* / Monograph-scale synthesis of faunal mortuary depositions (500 BC – AD 1050) in Rogaland, drawing on 128 grave assemblages; addresses how and why animal remains were incorporated into cremation and inhumation rites across the entire Iron Age sequence in southwest Norway. / Link: https://uis.brage.unit.no/uis-xmlui/handle/11250/3209504

## Multispecies Archaeology and Birds

**Feral Ecologies of the Human Deep Past: Multispecies Archaeology and Palaeo-Synanthropy** / *Shumon T. Hussain (2024) – Journal of the Royal Anthropological Institute 30(4), p. 1062* / Argues that the synanthropic nexus — species drawn into human ecological orbits — is a key analytical frame for multispecies archaeology, offering a theoretical scaffold applicable to human-animal entanglements in funerary and settlement contexts. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1111/1467-9655.14152

**Zooarchaeology of Managed, Captive, Tame, and Domestic Birds: Shifts in Human–Avian Relationships** / *L. Yeomans (2024/2025) – Journal of Archaeological Research* / Comprehensive review of archaeological evidence for shifting human–bird relationships across time and space, covering captivity, taming, domestication, and symbolic use — directly relevant to interpreting avian remains in Iron Age and Viking Age funerary contexts. / Link: https://doi.org/10.1007/s10814-024-09206-5

**Revisiting the Nabberör Boat Grave: A Multiproxy Analysis of a Vendel Period Multiple Burial on the Swedish Island of Öland** / *Ekengren, F., Macheridis, S., Wilhelmson, H., Jennbert, K. & Iregren, E. (2025, in press) – Praehistorische Zeitschrift* / Multiproxy reassessment (zooarchaeology, bioarchaeology, isotopes, aDNA) of an 8th-century boat grave containing 10–12 individuals, five dogs, two horses, one goat, sheep/goat, pig, and bird remains — a rare window into multispecies mortuary practice in the Vendel period. / Link: https://lup.lub.lu.se/search/publication/4ff213cf-9088-45a1-9c95-54d42c92f633
